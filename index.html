III. Relevant Tennessee Law

Stepparent Visitation – T.C.A. § 36-6-303

(a)(1) The court may order stepparent visitation when:

  • Child resided with stepparent ✓ (~3 years)
  • Stepparent provided financial support ✗ (minimal/none)
  • Stepparent had ongoing relationship ✓
  • Visitation is in child's best interest (to be determined)

(c) There is a rebuttable presumption that the biological parent's decision regarding visitation is NOT harmful to the child.

(d) A "best interest" finding only occurs after the petitioner proves "substantial harm" from the parent's decisions.

Key Precedent

Clark v. Johnson, 2018 WL 2411203 (Tenn. Ct. App. 2018): "It is well established in Tennessee that parents, barring a judicial finding of a risk of substantial harm to the children involved, enjoy a fundamental constitutional right to raise their children as they see fit."
In re Adoption of A.M.H., 215 S.W.3d 793 (Tenn. 2007): Parental rights are constitutionally protected and require clear and convincing evidence to overcome.

Guardian ad Litem – T.C.A. § 36-4-132(a)

Applies to "minor children of the marriage" — Daniel argues this excludes his children from prior marriage.

TN Supreme Court Rule 40(A), Section 3(b)

"[C]ourts should not routinely appoint guardians ad litem in custody proceedings. Rather, the court's discretion to appoint guardians ad litem shall be exercised sparingly."
Jessie's Core Legal Problem: Under T.C.A. § 36-6-303(c), there is a rebuttable presumption that Daniel's decision to deny visitation is NOT harmful. Jessie must prove: (1) that denying visitation causes substantial harm to the children, and (2) that visitation is in the children's best interest. Daniel's strongest argument: "The children have been fine for over a year without her. They're thriving."

V. Mock Q&A for High-Risk Questions

The following are scripted responses for the highest-risk questions. These should be practiced but delivered naturally, not memorized verbatim.

Question 1: "You never adopted Henry and Harper, correct?"

Q: You never adopted Henry and Harper, correct?

A: That's correct. A formal adoption was never completed.

Rationale: Don't fight the fact. A simple acknowledgment prevents follow-up attacks. If they press further:

Q: So you have no legal parental rights to these children?

A: I don't have the legal status of an adoptive parent. However, I functioned as their mother for three years with Daniel's full encouragement. I lived with them, cared for them daily, and they called me "Mommy."

Question 4: "Daniel says you avoided adoption to avoid responsibility. Is that accurate?"

Q: Daniel says you avoided adoption to avoid responsibility. Is that accurate?

A: No, that's not accurate. I was actively researching adoption options that would prioritize the children's best interests. I took on full responsibility for their daily care, their medical advocacy, and their developmental needs throughout our marriage—all without the legal protections adoption would have provided me.

Rationale: Directly deny the characterization, then pivot to evidence of actual responsibility taken. This reframes "no adoption" as her being vulnerable, not avoidant.

Question 6: "How much money did you contribute to the children's expenses?"

Q: How much money did you contribute to the children's expenses?

A: Our household operated with shared finances. I used the family credit card and bank account for the children's needs, which Daniel and I had agreed to. My direct monetary earnings during the marriage were limited because I was serving as the primary caregiver for the children—a role that allowed Daniel to manage his business and other affairs.

Q: So your direct financial contribution was essentially zero?

A: My direct cash deposits were minimal, that's true. But in a marriage, contributions aren't only measured in dollars. I contributed thousands of hours of caregiving, coordinated their medical care, managed the household, handled their school activities, and provided the daily parenting that these children needed.

Rationale: Acknowledge the financial reality but immediately reframe to non-monetary contributions. Don't be defensive about low income.

Question 11: "You claim Daniel is neglectful—do you have any CPS reports?"

Q: You claim Daniel is neglectful—do you have any CPS reports?

A: I did not file a CPS report. My concerns were about patterns of failing to follow through on professional recommendations—things like early intervention services that were recommended but not pursued. These aren't necessarily CPS-level issues, but they are concerns about the children's developmental wellbeing.

Q: So no government agency has ever found Daniel to be neglectful?

A: Not that I'm aware of.

Rationale: Concede the point cleanly, but distinguish between CPS-level abuse and developmental neglect. Don't overreach.

Question 13: "If Daniel was so neglectful, why did you stay for three years?"

Q: If Daniel was so neglectful, why did you stay for three years?

A: I stayed because I loved the children and I was committed to making the marriage work. I also believed that my presence was helping the children—that I was filling gaps in their care. Leaving would have meant abandoning children I had come to love as my own.

Rationale: This question is a classic trap. The answer reframes staying as commitment to the children, not tolerance of abuse. It also plants the seed that her presence was beneficial.

Question 14: "You claim a child screamed they wanted to die—did you report this to anyone?"

Q: You claim a child screamed they wanted to die—did you report this to anyone?

A: I discussed my concerns about the children's emotional state with Daniel on multiple occasions. I also raised these issues with our couples therapist, Heather Dryden. I advocated for the children to receive additional therapeutic support.

Q: But you didn't call the police or CPS?

A: No. At the time, I was working within the family to address these concerns. I believed that advocating for therapy and additional support was the appropriate response.

Rationale: Show that she did take action—just not formal reporting. Reference a witness (therapist) who can corroborate.

Question 18: "Daniel says the children are 'unharmed and thriving' since separation. Do you dispute this?"

Q: Daniel says the children are 'unharmed and thriving' since separation. Do you dispute this?

A: I hope the children are doing well. I haven't been allowed to see them for over a year, so I can't speak to their current state from personal observation. What I can say is that abruptly severing an attachment bond with a primary caregiver—which is what happened—is recognized by child development experts as harmful to children, even if the harm isn't immediately visible.

Rationale: Avoid claiming knowledge she doesn't have. Pivot to expert consensus on attachment disruption. This plants seeds for expert testimony later.

Question 20: "You continued living with Daniel for three years after this alleged abuse began?"

Q: You continued living with Daniel for three years after this alleged abuse began?

A: I did. Emotional abuse and coercive control often involve patterns that escalate gradually. I was committed to the marriage and to the children. I also want to be clear—I'm not claiming Daniel physically harmed me or the children. My concerns are about patterns of manipulation, broken promises, and decisions that weren't in the children's best interest.

Rationale: Don't overstate the abuse claims. Narrowing the scope makes her more credible and harder to attack.

Question 24: "On November 26, 2024, you refused to leave Daniel's home when asked, correct?"

Q: On November 26, 2024, you refused to leave Daniel's home when asked, correct?

A: That's not accurate. Daniel invited me into the home for a scheduled visit with the children. In the middle of that visit, he unexpectedly ordered me to leave. I did leave when he made clear he wanted me to go. The children were visibly distressed and clung to me as I departed.

Q: But you didn't leave immediately when first asked?

A: I was in the middle of a visit with children I had raised for three years. When it became clear Daniel was serious, I left. I did not cause a scene or refuse to go.

Rationale: Correct the mischaracterization, emphasize she left voluntarily, highlight the children's distress (which supports her case).

Question 28: "In over a year without you, have the children suffered any documented harm?"

Q: In over a year without you, have the children suffered any documented harm?

A: I don't have access to information about the children's current wellbeing because I've been completely cut off from them. I can't speak to documentation I haven't seen. What I know from child development research is that disrupting established attachment bonds causes harm—sometimes harm that manifests over time rather than immediately.

Rationale: Turn the lack of information into a point about being cut off. Reference general expertise without overclaiming specific knowledge.

Question 32: "You listed properties worth nearly $1.4 million as marital property—what did you contribute to purchase these?"

Q: You listed properties worth nearly $1.4 million as marital property—what did you contribute to purchase these?

A: During our marriage, Daniel consistently represented that all assets were jointly owned—he referred to them as "ours" and told me my name was on the accounts as proof of our equal partnership. I relied on those representations. As for my contributions, I maintained the household, provided full-time childcare, and managed the rental property—all of which enabled Daniel to manage his business affairs and preserve these assets.

Rationale: Reference Daniel's representations (potential estoppel argument), and emphasize that homemaker contributions have legal value in equitable distribution.

Question 38: "You want access to children you have no legal relationship with, whose father opposes it—why?"

Q: You want access to children you have no legal relationship with, whose father opposes it—why?

A: Because I love Henry and Harper, and they love me. I was their primary caregiver for three years. They called me "Mommy." Daniel encouraged that relationship and those bonds. Now he's asking the court to pretend those three years didn't happen. I'm asking for the opportunity to maintain a relationship that is important to these children's emotional wellbeing—not to replace Daniel as their parent, but to remain a stable, loving presence in their lives.

Rationale: This is the emotional core of her case. Lead with love, reference the children's perspective, and make clear she's not seeking to displace Daniel.

VI. Mock Q&A for Medium-Risk Questions

These questions are less dangerous but still require careful handling. They often set up follow-up attacks or try to establish facts that will be used later.

Category 1: Adoption Steps

Question 3: "What steps did you actually take toward adoption?"

Q: What steps did you actually take toward adoption?

A: I researched Tennessee adoption law, including the requirements for stepparent adoption. I looked into what would be required given that the children's biological mother was deceased. I discussed with Daniel at various points how to approach this in a way that would honor Rachel's memory while also providing the children with legal security.

Q: Did you ever hire an attorney for adoption?

A: No, we did not get to that stage.

Q: Did you ever file any paperwork with the court?

A: No.

Rationale: Be honest about what was and wasn't done. The research and conversations show intent. Don't overstate or invent steps that didn't happen.

Category 2: Income & Financial Dependence

Question 7: "You earned approximately $2,600 in 2022, correct?"

Q: You earned approximately $2,600 in 2022, correct?

A: That's approximately correct for my self-employment income that year, yes.

Q: And in 2023, it was even less—about $1,600?

A: That sounds right. My direct earning capacity was limited because I was serving as the primary caregiver in the household.

Rationale: Confirm the numbers matter-of-factly. Don't be defensive. The caregiver framing explains the low income without excuses.

Question 9: "You used Daniel's credit card for purchases, correct?"

Q: You used Daniel's credit card for purchases, correct?

A: I was an authorized user on a family credit card, yes. Daniel added me to that account and encouraged me to use it for household and children's expenses. That was our agreed arrangement.

Q: But the account was in his name and paid from his funds?

A: The account originated with Daniel, yes. It was paid from accounts we both had access to, which Daniel had described as our shared family finances.

Rationale: Emphasize this was an agreed arrangement, not her taking advantage. Reference Daniel's representations about shared finances.

Category 3: Basic Care Questions

Question 16: "The children are enrolled in school, correct?"

Q: The children are enrolled in school, correct?

A: Yes, they were enrolled in school during our marriage. I assume they still are, though I have no current information about them.

Rationale: Simple confirmation. The second sentence subtly highlights her being cut off from information.

Question 17: "They have a pediatrician, correct?"

Q: They have a pediatrician, correct?

A: Yes. During our marriage, I was often the one who scheduled and took them to their appointments.

Rationale: Confirm the fact but pivot to her involvement. This supports her caregiver role.

Category 4: Abuse Claims & Documentation

Question 19: "You allege emotional abuse—did you ever seek a protection order?"

Q: You allege emotional abuse—did you ever seek a protection order?

A: No, I did not. The patterns I experienced were more subtle—manipulation, broken promises, financial control, unpredictable emotional responses. These aren't the kinds of things that typically result in protection orders, but they made for an unhealthy marriage.

Rationale: Acknowledge the lack of formal action while explaining why. Don't overstate the abuse claims.

Question 22: "You claim your physical symptoms improved after separation—do you have medical records?"

Q: You claim your physical symptoms improved after separation—do you have medical records documenting this?

A: I can speak to my own experience. I had digestive issues and other stress-related symptoms during the marriage that improved significantly after the separation. Whether I have specific medical records documenting the before and after, I would need to check with my healthcare providers.

Q: So you don't have medical documentation proving the marriage caused these symptoms?

A: I'm not claiming to have medical proof of causation. I'm describing my personal experience of stress-related symptoms that improved when I was no longer in the marital environment.

Rationale: Don't overclaim. Stick to personal experience without asserting medical proof you may not have.

Question 23: "Daniel invited you for visits after separation, correct? Does an abuser do that?"

Q: Daniel invited you for visits with the children after separation, correct?

A: He did allow some visits initially, yes.

Q: Does an abuser typically invite their victim to visit?

A: I think the question oversimplifies the dynamics. Daniel's behavior wasn't consistent—he would offer visits, then revoke them. He would invite me over, then suddenly order me to leave. That unpredictability was part of the pattern I experienced.

Rationale: Don't take the bait on the rhetorical question. Reframe to show the inconsistency, which actually supports her claims.

Category 5: The Separation Details

Question 25: "Daniel had to ask you multiple times to leave?"

Q: Daniel had to ask you multiple times to leave?

A: I don't recall the exact number of times. What I remember is that he invited me for a visit, the visit was proceeding normally, and then he suddenly demanded I leave in the middle of my time with the children. I was surprised by the abruptness, but I did leave.

Rationale: "I don't recall the exact number" is honest. Focus on the abruptness and the fact that she complied.

Question 27: "Since November 2024, you have had no contact with the children, correct?"

Q: Since November 2024, you have had no contact with the children, correct?

A: That's correct. Daniel has not permitted any contact since that date.

Q: Have you attempted to contact them?

A: I've requested visitation through my attorney. I've respected the legal process rather than attempting contact that Daniel has made clear he doesn't want.

Rationale: Confirm the timeline. Show she's being respectful of legal boundaries, not aggressive or boundary-violating.

Category 6: Property & Financial Claims

Question 29: "You claim Daniel promised equal partnership—do you have that in writing?"

Q: You claim Daniel promised you an equal partnership in the marriage—do you have that in writing?

A: Not in a formal written agreement, no. But Daniel repeatedly told me throughout our relationship and marriage that we were equal partners, that assets were "ours," and that my name on accounts proved our shared ownership. I relied on those representations.

Q: So it's your word against his?

A: These weren't private conversations. Daniel made statements about our equal partnership to friends and family. His public persona was built around egalitarian relationships. And his actions during the marriage—adding me to accounts, encouraging me to use family finances—were consistent with those representations until he changed his position during the divorce.

Rationale: Acknowledge lack of written documentation but point to consistent behavior and potential witnesses.

Question 30: "You allege Daniel 'went to the bank in secrecy'—how do you know if it was secret?"

Q: You allege Daniel "went to the bank in secrecy"—how do you know if it was secret?

A: He didn't tell me he was going or what he was doing there. I learned about it afterward when he mentioned that my name was on accounts. The way it was presented suggested he had done something to benefit us both, but I later learned I was only a signatory, not a joint owner. If it wasn't secret, I would have expected to be involved in those decisions.

Rationale: Explain what "secret" means in context—lack of transparency and involvement, not necessarily hiding.

Question 31: "In your interrogatories, you couldn't recall transferring any property—but the condo is in your name?"

Q: In your interrogatories, you said you couldn't recall transferring any property. But the condo at Stringers Ridge is in your name, isn't it?

A: The condo was purchased during the marriage and titled in my name, yes. I didn't transfer it to anyone. I understood the question to be asking about transfers I made, not property that was titled to me.

Q: So you do have property in your name worth approximately $229,000?

A: The condo is titled in my name, yes. It was purchased with marital funds during the marriage.

Rationale: Clarify the interrogatory interpretation. Don't let them suggest you were being deceptive.

Category 7: Motive Questions

Question 34: "You're seeking alimony from Daniel, correct?"

Q: You're seeking alimony from Daniel, correct?

A: My counter-complaint includes a request for spousal support, yes. I significantly reduced my earning capacity during the marriage to serve as the primary caregiver for Daniel's children, and Tennessee law recognizes that contribution.

Rationale: Confirm directly. Frame it as legally appropriate, not opportunistic.

Question 35: "Before full-time employment in July 2025, you were largely financially dependent on Daniel?"

Q: Before your full-time employment in July 2025, you were largely financially dependent on Daniel, weren't you?

A: During the marriage, we operated as a family unit with shared finances. Daniel's income supported the household while I provided full-time caregiving. After the separation, I worked to become financially independent and secured full-time employment as soon as I could.

Rationale: Reframe "dependence" as the normal arrangement in a single-income household. Highlight her efforts toward independence post-separation.

Question 36: "If you obtain visitation, will you seek child support?"

Q: If you obtain visitation with these children, will you seek child support?

A: I'm not seeking custody or child support. I'm seeking the opportunity to maintain a relationship with children I helped raise. This is about the children's emotional wellbeing, not financial support.

Rationale: Clearly separate visitation from financial claims. This undermines the "financial motive" argument.

Question 37: "This Guardian ad Litem motion—who would pay for that?"

Q: This Guardian ad Litem motion—who would pay for that if the court grants it?

A: Typically the cost would be shared by the parties, allocated by the court based on ability to pay. I understand it's an expense. But the purpose of a Guardian ad Litem is to ensure the children's interests are independently represented. Given the conflict between Daniel and me about what's best for the children, I believe that independent voice is important.

Rationale: Acknowledge the cost but pivot to the children's interests. This shows she's not frivolously requesting it.

VII. Deposition Ground Rules

DO DON'T
Answer only what's asked Volunteer additional information
Say "I don't recall" if true Guess or speculate
Stay calm and factual Get emotional or defensive
Ask for clarification on confusing questions Assume you understand compound questions
Take breaks if needed Let fatigue cause mistakes
Listen to the entire question before answering Interrupt or anticipate
Correct mistakes immediately Let inaccurate statements stand
Look at documents carefully before answering about them React to documents without reading them

Common Traps to Avoid

The Silence Trap: After you answer, opposing counsel stays quiet. Don't fill the silence with more information.

The Friendly Approach: Lentz may start cordial to lower your guard. Stay consistent regardless of tone.

The Document Surprise: "I'm showing you Exhibit A..." Stay calm, read carefully, don't react hastily.

The "Isn't it true" Setup: These are leading questions designed to lock in harmful admissions. It's okay to say "That's not accurate" or "That's incomplete."

The Summary Trap: "So what you're saying is..." followed by a distorted version of your testimony. Correct any mischaracterization immediately.

Jessie's best path forward is demonstrating that the established attachment bond, the children's own expressed wishes, and expert opinion on attachment disruption collectively meet the "extraordinary circumstances" standard.
